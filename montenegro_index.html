Before You Go

The Essentials

Everything you need before leaving the hotel.

Rent a car — it makes a huge differenceRoads are winding but manageable. The scenery is extraordinary. Many of the best places are impossible without one.
Kotor is the best base for everythingPerast 20 min. Budva 30 min. Cetinje 40 min. Lake Skadar 1 hr. Everything reachable in a day.
Taxis are cheap and reliable in townsAgree the fare before getting in if no meter. Most drivers in tourist areas speak basic English.
Currency is Euro (€) — despite not being in the EUCards accepted in most hotels and larger restaurants. Carry cash for small bakeries, boats, and markets.
Montenegro is genuinely cheapCoffee €1–1.50. Beer €2–3. Full meal at a konoba €8–15. Budget accommodation €30–50/night.
Best time to visit: May, June, SeptemberJuly–August: hot, crowded, expensive. May, June, September: warm, quieter, prices drop significantly.
The coast is Mediterranean — the mountains are AlpineKotor and the bay can be hot in summer. Durmitor and Cetinje are significantly cooler. Pack for both if heading inland.
Good walking shoes are essentialKotor's city walls are 1,355 steps. Village roads are uneven stone. Flip flops will not cut it.
Sun protection is non-negotiable on the coastThe bay creates a heat trap in summer. The sun reflects off the water. Bring high-factor sunscreen.
Get an eSIM before you flyAiralo sells Montenegro eSIMs. Install on the plane, works on landing.
Download Google Maps offline before you goSignal in the mountains is patchy. Download Montenegro on Google Maps offline before you travel.

Mark as visited

Cetinje was Montenegro's royal capital, set in a high plateau completely different from the coast. Small, quiet, almost untouched. The monastery holds what the church claims is the mummified hand of John the Baptist.

Curiosity'Montenegro' means 'Black Mountain' in Venetian Italian — named by traders who saw the dark outline of Mount Lovćen from the sea.

Insider Tips

Good to Know

The things guidebooks either miss or refuse to say.

Look for a konoba — the traditional family tavernAvoid anywhere with photos on the menu or someone outside pulling you in. A handwritten menu is always the right choice.
Lamb under the bell (ispod sača) is the dishA cast iron bell covers the meat with embers on top. Slow cooked for hours. If a konoba has it, order it. No hesitation.
Njeguški pršut and cheese are worth seeking outSmoke-cured ham from Njeguši village. With local cheese and wine — the definitive Montenegrin starter.
Vranac is Montenegro's own red grape — always order localDark, full-bodied, slightly dry. Very good and cheap — €2–4 per glass. Ask for domaće vino in any konoba.
Rakija will be offered constantly — do not refuse the first glassLocal fruit brandy. Refusing is considered rude. Sip it, do not shoot it. Say živjeli and you are fine.
The bay road is narrow and the cruise ship crowds are realKotor fills completely at midday when cruise ships dock. Go to the walls very early or late afternoon. Midday is a mistake.
The ferry across the bay saves 30 minutesLepetane to Kamenari — runs frequently, costs a few euros, cuts the bay drive significantly. Worth using if heading south.
The viewpoint above Kotor is free and extraordinaryThe mountain road above Kotor has switchbacks with pull-offs looking directly down into the entire bay. Stop at any of them.
Never eat at restaurants immediately next to the old town entranceRestaurants on the main gate square in Kotor are priced for cruise passengers. Walk two streets in.
Never underestimate driving times on mountain roadsGoogle Maps assumes normal roads. Mountain roads are narrow and winding. Add 30–40% to any estimated time.
